The Data, Information, Knowledge, and Wisdom (DIKW) hierarchy is a model that illustrates how raw data can be transformed into actionable wisdom. Each level builds upon the previous one, adding context and understanding. Knowledge isn't a single entity; it exists in various forms. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for fields like education, artificial intelligence, and management. Below, we explore two fundamental dichotomies: knowledge that is easily documented versus knowledge gained from experience, and knowledge derived from logic versus knowledge from observation.

Explicit vs. Tacit Knowledge

Explicit Knowledge

This is knowledge that is easily articulated, documented, and shared. It's often codified in manuals, databases, textbooks, and procedures. It's the "know-what" that can be written down.

Examples:

  • A user manual for a smartphone
  • A recipe for a cake
  • A list of facts about the solar system

Tacit Knowledge

This is personal, experience-based knowledge that is difficult to articulate or write down. It's the "know-how" or intuition transferred through observation and practice.

Examples:

  • Knowing how to ride a bike
  • A chef's instinct for balancing flavors
  • Reading a customer's non-verbal cues

A Priori vs. A Posteriori Knowledge

A Priori Knowledge

Knowledge that is independent of sensory experience. It is justified by pure reason, logic, or deduction. These truths do not require empirical observation to be known.

Examples:

  • All bachelors are unmarried men.
  • The sum of a triangle's angles is 180Â°.
  • 2 + 2 = 4

A Posteriori Knowledge

Knowledge that is dependent on sensory experience or empirical evidence. It is gained through observation, experimentation, and personal experience.

Examples:

  • The sky is blue.
  • A dog has four legs.
  • The Earth revolves around the Sun.

While the concepts themselves are ancient, their formal combination into the DIKW hierarchy is a relatively modern construct in information science. This section provides a brief overview of its origins and the thinkers who shaped it, offering context to the model's enduring influence and its occasional critiques.

Origins of the Hierarchy

The model is most famously attributed to organizational theorist Russell L. Ackoff in his 1989 paper, "From Data to Wisdom." Ackoff's work articulated the hierarchy in a way that resonated with information science and knowledge management practitioners.

However, he was not the first to propose similar ideas. Early roots can be traced to philosophers and thinkers who explored the progression from raw perception to understanding. For instance, T.S. Eliot's 1934 poem "Choruses from the Rock" contains lines that allude to a similar progression:

"Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge? Where is the knowledge we have lost in information?"

While the DIKW model is widely used, it has also faced criticism for being too simplistic or linear. However, its enduring legacy lies in its ability to provide a clear, intuitive framework for thinking about the process of converting raw data into actionable insight and, ultimately, sound judgment.
