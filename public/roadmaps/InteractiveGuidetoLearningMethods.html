Interactive Guide to Learning Methods

Discover evidence-based learning techniques that help you learn faster and retain information better.

Active Recall

Effectiveness: Very High

What it is: Testing yourself on material without looking at the source.

How to use: Close your notes and try to write down everything you remember. Use flashcards or practice questions.

Why it works: Forces your brain to retrieve information, strengthening neural pathways.

Spaced Repetition

Effectiveness: Very High

What it is: Reviewing material at increasing intervals over time.

How to use: Review new material after 1 day, then 3 days, then 1 week, then 2 weeks, etc.

Why it works: Exploits the spacing effect to move information into long-term memory.

Feynman Technique

Effectiveness: High

What it is: Explaining concepts in simple terms as if teaching someone else.

How to use: Choose a concept, explain it simply, identify gaps, and refine your explanation.

Why it works: Reveals gaps in understanding and forces you to think clearly about concepts.

Interleaving

Effectiveness: High

What it is: Mixing different types of problems or topics in a single study session.

How to use: Instead of studying one topic for hours, switch between related topics every 20-30 minutes.

Why it works: Improves discrimination between concepts and enhances transfer of learning.

Elaborative Interrogation

Effectiveness: Medium-High

What it is: Asking "why" and "how" questions about the material.

How to use: For each fact or concept, ask yourself why it's true and how it connects to other ideas.

Why it works: Creates meaningful connections and deeper understanding.

Creating Your Learning Plan

  1. Choose 2-3 techniques that appeal to you
  2. Start with active recall and spaced repetition
  3. Experiment with different combinations
  4. Track your progress and adjust as needed
